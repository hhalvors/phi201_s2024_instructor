Extending the language

Subject-predicate sentences

In propositional logic, the basic unit of analysis is the atomic sentence. But in predicate logic, atomic sentences can be broken down into sub-sentential components. For example, the atomic sentence “Alice is Portuguese” is built out of the name “Alice” and the predicate “…is Portuguese”. We symbolize names by lowercase letters, so in this case we could choose a to represent “Alice”. We represent predicates by capital letters, so in this case we could choose P to represent “…is Portuguese”. Putting the two components together, the sentence “Alice is Portuguese” can be represented by the compound symbol Pa.

Variables and formulas

You will be familiar with variables from, say, high school algebra. For some reason or other, people tend to use the letters x, y, z for variables, and we will usually do the same. But that’s just a convention. The role of variables is something that first became clarified by symbolic logic.

While premises and conclusions are always statements, we have need for an auxiliary notion of a formula. A formula doesn’t necessarily say something. For example, if P is a predicate symbol, then Px is a formula, but not a statement. As a generall rule, a formula is something that can become a statement if variables are replaced by names.
